Gear

Sep 19, 2018

Here is a list of recommended gear for our trip in November. Feel free to contact one of us if you have specific questions about these items, or if you need a hand getting ahold of a few of them. - Jason

Backpack. For a two-nighter, a 40-liter pack is around the right size. Itâ€™s important that the pack fit comfortably and has a padded waist strap allowing the weight to shift to your hips.

Clothing. Think in layers. A couple pairs of underwear and socks, a pair of hiking shorts and/or pants, a t-shirt (quick-drying Capilene or wool preferably), a long-sleeve shirt (again Capilene or wool), and a fleece-type pullover for warmth in camp. And, depending on the weather, a knit hat for sleeping, and gloves.

Dining Utensils. Everyone needs a plate or bowl, a mug, and a spork/fork/spoon.

First Aid. We will bring a first-aid kit for the group.

Flashlight. A Petzl Tikka-type headlamp is great for hiking in the dark and for reading in the tent.

Food. We will be sharing a meal in the evenings, which we will purchase, organize, and distribute beforehand. You are responsible for packing your own breakfasts and snacks. Go with what works for you, ie., energy bars (there are many to choose from these days), cheese, jerkey, Pop Tarts, candy bars, trail mix, nuts, dried fruit, etc. We will make sure there is coffee for all each morning as well!

Hiking Shoes. You know your feet. Wear what works for you. Often, trail-running shoes can be just as good, if not better, than traditional hiking boots.

Rain Gear. Rain pants are optional, but a hooded, reliable GoreTex/eVent-type rain jacket is a must.

Shelter. Tents, bivy sacks, and hammocks all work well. Bring one of your choosing. We may have a couple to spare. Let me know if you would like to borrow one.

Sleeping Bag. It is possible that the temperature will get low in early November. Bring a sleeping bag (synthetic or down) that will keep you warm at least to 30 degrees.

Sleeping Pad. A closed-cell foam pad is a reliable choice, such as the RidgeRest and Z Lite. These double as comfortable seating around camp. There are some nice lightweight inflatable choices out there as well these days, if you need a little more padding.

Toiletries. Other than the personal items you need, ie., toothbrush, medication., etc., make sure to pack some toilet paper, hand sanitizer, bug dope, and sunscreen as well.

Water Bottles. Everyone needs to bring two, 2-liter water bottles.

Water Shoes. Lightweight Crocks or something similar will help you get across the streams and make you more comfortable in camp.
